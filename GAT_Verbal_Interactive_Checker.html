Passage 1 (Questions 55–59)

Many people believe that multitasking makes them more productive, especially when they face a large number of assignments and strict deadlines. They often imagine that handling several tasks at the same time will help them finish everything more quickly and impress their teachers or employers. As a result, students may listen to music, answer messages, and work on homework simultaneously, thinking they are using their time wisely. However, research in psychology suggests the opposite. Studies indicate that the human brain is not designed to focus deeply on several demanding tasks at once. When individuals try to concentrate on more than one task, their brains must constantly switch from one activity to another. A student, for example, may move from a message on the phone to a paragraph in a textbook, then quickly check a social‑media notification before returning to the assignment. Each time this switch happens, the brain needs a moment to remember what it was doing and to decide what to do next. Although this pause is short, it is repeated many times, which quietly wastes time and mental energy. This frequent switching has several negative effects. People are more likely to lose track of their progress, repeat steps they have already completed, and make unnecessary mistakes. Their work may also feel more stressful because they never reach a state of deep concentration. In contrast, focusing on a single important task allows the brain to work more deeply and consistently, which usually leads to higher‑quality results and a calmer feeling. For this reason, experts often recommend that students turn off notifications, close unnecessary tabs, and create a quiet, organized study space when working on major assignments, especially before important exams. By doing one thing at a time, students can often finish faster and perform better than when they try to multitask.

Passage 2 (Questions 60–64)

Urban farming is becoming increasingly popular in cities around the world, from small industrial towns to large international capitals. Instead of buying all their food from supermarkets, some city residents grow vegetables, fruits, and herbs on balconies, rooftops, and in small community gardens that are shared by several families. These spaces are often created in unused areas, such as the roofs of apartment buildings, the corners of schoolyards, or narrow empty lots between houses. For many people, urban farming provides fresher produce, reduces transportation costs, and offers a pleasant break from concrete streets and crowded traffic. One of the main challenges of farming in the city is the lack of open land. Apartments are stacked on top of one another, and outdoor space is limited and expensive. To solve this problem, urban farmers use creative methods to grow more plants in less space. Vertical gardens, for example, allow plants to grow up walls or on tall frames rather than spreading out across the ground. Hanging baskets and containers placed on windowsills or balconies make it possible to grow herbs and small vegetables even in very small areas. Some projects recycle materials such as plastic bottles, wooden boxes, or old metal cans to create low‑cost containers. Urban farming offers benefits beyond fresh food. Community gardens bring neighbors together and help build stronger relationships among residents who might not otherwise meet. Schools sometimes organize visits to these gardens so that students can see how food is produced and understand how soil, water, and sunlight work together to support plant life. In this way, urban farms provide valuable lessons about nutrition, sustainability, and the environment. Even if a single rooftop garden cannot feed an entire city, it can still improve the quality of life for the people who plant, care for, and enjoy it.

Passage 3 (Questions 65–68)

Many companies are beginning to allow employees to work from home for part of the week while spending the rest of their time in the office. This mixed arrangement, often called a “hybrid” model, has become more common as communication technology has improved. High‑speed Internet connections, cloud‑storage services, and video‑conferencing tools make it possible for teams to share documents and hold meetings online, even when members are in different cities or countries. For office workers, the option to work from home can offer several advantages. One clear benefit is the reduction of daily travel. Employees who no longer need to commute to the office every day may save both time and money. Instead of spending long hours in traffic or on crowded buses, they can use that time to rest, exercise, or focus on their tasks. Working from home can also provide more flexibility in managing the day. Some employees may prefer to start earlier in the morning, while others feel more productive in the evening, as long as they complete their work and attend important online meetings. However, remote work also presents challenges. Some employees feel isolated from their colleagues and miss informal conversations that naturally happen in the office, such as short talks during breaks. Managers may find it more difficult to monitor performance or to notice when someone is struggling with a task. In addition, not every home is a suitable work environment; noise, family responsibilities, or limited space can all reduce productivity. To succeed with remote or hybrid work, both employers and employees must establish clear expectations, maintain regular communication through emails and virtual meetings, and create a productive work environment at home that includes a quiet workspace and a stable routine.
