Passage 1: Attention and Study Quality (Questions 29–38)

In many discussions about academic success, “study time” is treated as the decisive variable: the more hours a student spends with a textbook, the better the outcome. Yet educational research increasingly distinguishes between time-on-task and quality of attention. A learner may sit at a desk for three hours while switching between tabs, responding to notifications, and “half reading” assigned pages. The clock records diligence, but the mind registers fragmentation. Cognitive scientists describe attention as a limited resource. When it is repeatedly divided, the brain pays a “switching cost” each time it reorients to a new task. That cost is usually invisible: a few seconds to recover context, a slight decline in comprehension, and a subtle increase in fatigue. Over dozens of interruptions, the small costs accumulate into meaningful loss. In controlled studies, students who work continuously on one demanding task often finish sooner and remember more than students who alternate rapidly between tasks. However, the problem is not merely the presence of technology. Switching can happen without a phone: daydreaming, worrying about unrelated obligations, or studying in a noisy space. The core issue is sustained engagement. Deep comprehension—especially of dense texts—requires building a mental model, connecting new information to prior knowledge, and monitoring one’s own understanding. These processes are difficult to perform when attention is continually reset. To address this, educators have promoted practical strategies that aim to protect focus without demanding impossible self-discipline. One approach is “pre-commitment”: removing temptations before studying begins, such as turning off notifications, placing the phone in another room, or using website blockers for a set period. Another is “active retrieval,” which replaces passive rereading with self-testing. Even short quizzes force the mind to reconstruct information, revealing gaps that rereading can hide. Finally, “spaced practice” distributes learning across multiple sessions, improving long-term retention compared with last-minute cramming. The implication is not that time is irrelevant, but that hours are a weak proxy for learning when attention is scattered. Students who want to improve their performance should therefore measure progress not only by duration, but by the clarity of recall, the ability to explain concepts, and the consistency of focused practice.

Passage 2: Urban Growth and Livability (Questions 39–48)

Cities are often described as engines of opportunity, but their growth creates a persistent dilemma: how to expand without making daily life less livable. When development spreads outward into low-density suburbs, residents frequently become dependent on private cars. Commutes lengthen, traffic increases, and public services—such as water, electricity, and road maintenance—must be extended over larger areas. The result can be higher costs for governments and households alike, even if the suburbs initially appear affordable. Urban planners have responded with a range of strategies, many of which share a central idea: concentrate growth rather than disperse it. Mixed-use zoning encourages housing, shops, and workplaces to coexist within the same neighborhoods, reducing the need for long trips. Transit-oriented development clusters new buildings near bus or rail stations so that public transportation becomes a realistic alternative to driving. Similarly, “complete streets” redesign roads to serve pedestrians and cyclists as well as cars, promoting safer and more diverse mobility. Yet concentrating growth raises its own concerns. Densification can increase rents if housing supply does not keep up with demand, and some residents fear that taller buildings will change the character of established communities. Moreover, not all forms of density are equal. A neighborhood packed with parking lots and wide highways may be dense in population but still hostile to walking. The details of design—sidewalks, shade, street-level shops, and reliable transit—often determine whether density feels vibrant or oppressive. The debate is sometimes framed as a choice between “sprawl” and “density,” but the most realistic path is more nuanced. Cities can preserve green space and reduce congestion while still building new housing, provided they invest in public transport, reform zoning rules, and coordinate development across metropolitan regions. Ultimately, the goal is not growth for its own sake, but a built environment that supports economic activity without sacrificing health, affordability, and time.

Passage 3: AI Decision-Making and Fairness (Questions 49–58)

As artificial intelligence systems become more capable, organizations increasingly delegate decisions to algorithms: whom to hire, which transactions to flag, or how to prioritize patients on waiting lists. Supporters highlight speed and consistency. Unlike humans, a program does not grow tired, forget criteria, or let personal feelings influence judgment. But critics argue that algorithmic consistency can be misleading. If an automated system makes the same biased decision every time, it becomes reliably unfair. Bias often enters through data. Training sets capture historical patterns, including discriminatory ones. When a hiring model is trained on past decisions, it may “learn” to prefer profiles that resemble those previously selected, even if the selection reflected unequal access to education or opportunity. In finance, credit models can correlate repayment with neighborhood or employment history in ways that indirectly track race or class, producing outcomes that look neutral but reinforce inequality. Because of these risks, many researchers call for transparency and accountability. Transparency includes clearer documentation of how a model was built, which variables it uses, and how performance differs across groups. Accountability concerns who bears responsibility when an algorithm causes harm. The developer may argue that the client misused the tool; the client may argue that the system was a “black box” sold as reliable. Without shared standards, responsibility can be shifted until it disappears. Still, the debate is not simply pro- or anti-AI. Algorithms can also reduce unfairness when used carefully. In some contexts, structured models outperform human judgment that is distorted by prejudice or inconsistency. The crucial condition is governance: regular auditing, mechanisms for appeal, and an institutional willingness to revise or abandon models that fail ethical tests. In practice, responsible AI resembles responsible medicine: powerful, beneficial, and dangerous when used without oversight.

Passage 4: Peer Review, Preprints, and Misinformation (Questions 59–68)

Scientific claims earn public trust not only because they are produced by experts, but because they pass through systems designed to detect error. Peer review—evaluation by independent specialists before publication—remains one of the best-known safeguards. Reviewers assess whether methods are appropriate, whether data supports conclusions, and whether alternative explanations have been considered. Although the process is imperfect, it is meant to reduce the likelihood that flawed work becomes accepted as fact. In the digital era, however, the pathway from research to public belief has become faster and less filtered. Preprint servers allow researchers to share findings before peer review, which can accelerate collaboration and invite early feedback. At the same time, non-specialists may treat preliminary results as settled truth. Social platforms amplify the problem: algorithms often reward content that is simple, emotional, or controversial, even when scientific conclusions are cautious and conditional. Critics of peer review point out that it can be biased, slow, and conservative. Novel ideas may face higher skepticism, and reviewers may disagree strongly about what counts as sufficient evidence. Yet abandoning peer review would remove an important checkpoint precisely when misinformation spreads quickly. A more productive response is reform. Proposals include open peer review (where identities and reports are published), stronger requirements for data sharing, and clearer labeling of preprints as unverified. Ultimately, the challenge is not to eliminate uncertainty—science often advances by revising earlier views—but to communicate uncertainty honestly. When the public understands that “evidence suggests” is not the same as “we know,” trust can become more resilient. In that sense, scientific integrity depends as much on transparent communication as on technical expertise.
