Passage 1: Economic Rationality and Behavioral Evidence (Questions 29–38)

Modern economic theory often models individuals as rational maximizers of utility. Within this framework, choices are assumed to reflect stable preferences ordered consistently across contexts. Yet a broad body of behavioral research documents systematic departures from these assumptions. Individuals overweight immediate rewards, exhibit loss aversion, and respond to framing effects in which equivalent options appear more or less attractive depending on how they are presented. These regularities have prompted debate not merely about isolated “biases” but about the adequacy of the rational-actor model as the backbone of explanation. Critics argue that the model is descriptively inaccurate: people are neither omniscient nor internally consistent, and their reasoning is shaped by limited attention and incomplete information. Defenders respond that the model’s value lies less in psychological realism than in predictive simplicity. Abstraction can be an asset: by ignoring some cognitive complexity, economists derive tractable models that generate clear, testable hypotheses. The dispute, therefore, concerns the trade-off between descriptive fidelity and analytical usefulness. However, abandoning rationality entirely risks dissolving explanation into a list of context-specific heuristics. If every deviation is treated as idiosyncratic, coherent theory becomes elusive. Some scholars therefore advocate bounded rationality, which preserves purposive behavior while acknowledging computational and informational limits. Under this view, individuals aim to optimize, but they do so with shortcuts and under constraints; the task for theory is to specify when those constraints matter and how they systematically shape outcomes. The methodological implications are practical as well as philosophical. In policy design, small behavioral frictions—defaults, salience, timing—can have outsized effects, so realism may matter. In large-scale market analysis, by contrast, aggregation may wash out some individual quirks, allowing simplified assumptions to remain informative. Ultimately, the legitimacy of a model may depend less on whether it mirrors cognition and more on whether it answers the question being asked, while remaining honest about its limits.

Passage 2: Paradigms, Anomalies, and Scientific Change (Questions 39–48)

Scientific revolutions are often narrated as triumphs of evidence, in which new data compels the abandonment of old ideas. Yet many historians and philosophers of science argue that the transition is rarely so straightforward. Within an established paradigm, anomalies are commonly treated as puzzles to be resolved rather than as immediate refutations. Researchers may adjust auxiliary assumptions, refine instruments, or reinterpret results so that the broader framework remains intact. In this phase, persistence is rational: discarding a successful paradigm too quickly would make inquiry unstable. Crisis emerges only when anomalies accumulate beyond what the paradigm can plausibly absorb, or when a rival framework offers an alternative that organizes the same evidence more coherently. Crucially, rival paradigms can be incommensurable. They differ not only in conclusions but also in foundational assumptions, key terms, and standards of methodological adequacy. What counts as a decisive test under one paradigm may be dismissed as irrelevant under another, because the two frameworks ask different questions or measure success by different criteria. For this reason, paradigm change involves conceptual reorientation as well as empirical revision. Scientists must learn to “see” phenomena differently: categories shift, problems are redescribed, and familiar results take on new meaning. This does not entail that science is arbitrary, but it does imply that rational evaluation is partly internal to a framework. Evidence constrains theory, yet the interpretation of evidence is guided by background commitments about what is worth measuring and how to measure it. Sociological forces can amplify these dynamics. Professional networks, institutional incentives, publication norms, and generational turnover influence which ideas receive attention and which careers flourish. Novel approaches may face skepticism not because they are false, but because they disrupt established training and tools. Still, sociological influence does not erase the role of reasons; rather, it shows that reasons are assessed within communities with histories, incentives, and limited time. On this view, scientific progress is neither mere accumulation nor mere fashion, but a complex interaction between data, concepts, and institutions.

Passage 3: Representation and Democratic Legitimacy (Questions 49–58)

Modern democracies justify political authority by appealing to representation: citizens authorize officials to decide on their behalf. Yet representation introduces distance between public preferences and public policy. Elections provide authorization, but authorization does not guarantee alignment. Officials may face incentives—party discipline, donor influence, bureaucratic constraints—that pull them away from median voter preferences. Moreover, electoral accountability is episodic. Between elections, citizens often have limited leverage, and by the time they vote again, policy consequences may be difficult to trace to particular decisions. Defenders of representative institutions respond that this distance is not purely a defect. Institutional mediation can protect minority rights, reduce volatility, and enable long-term planning that would be difficult under constant plebiscites. A system that mirrors opinion instantly may also mirror misinformation, panic, or temporary anger. From this perspective, a measure of insulation is a feature that stabilizes governance, provided that channels of correction remain credible. Deliberative theorists propose supplementing electoral mechanisms with participatory forums—citizens’ assemblies, deliberative polls, structured public hearings—designed to improve the quality of collective judgment. The aim is not simply to count preferences but to refine them through exposure to evidence and argument. Skeptics reply that participation without expertise can empower demagogic appeals, while participation with heavy expert control can replicate elitism under a different name. The challenge is to design participation that is informed without being captured. The legitimacy question therefore becomes multidimensional. Responsiveness matters, but so do rights, competence, and stability. A democracy may fail if it ignores citizens, but it may also fail if it treats momentary opinion as sovereign. Legitimate authority, on this view, depends on institutions that transform inputs—preferences, grievances, information—into decisions through procedures that citizens can recognize as fair, even when outcomes disappoint them.

Passage 4: Language, Cognition, and Interaction (Questions 59–68)

Language does not merely transmit thought; it can shape it by directing attention to certain distinctions while leaving others less salient. Linguists and psychologists have long debated how far this influence extends. Strong versions of linguistic determinism claim that grammar and vocabulary rigidly structure cognition, making some concepts difficult to grasp unless a language encodes them. Weaker interactionist views, by contrast, hold that language nudges habitual perception and memory without imprisoning the mind. Evidence for interaction often comes from tasks that measure attention and categorization. Speakers of languages that obligatorily mark spatial relations may become faster at noticing those relations, not because others cannot perceive them, but because their language trains repeated attention. Similarly, color- label studies suggest that naming can sharpen boundary judgments near category edges. Yet these effects are usually modest and context-sensitive, and they coexist with cognitive universals: humans across communities track number, agency, and causal relations even when languages differ widely. The debate therefore shifts from an all-or-nothing question to one of degree and mechanism. How does language interact with perception—through memory rehearsal, attentional habits, or social coordination? And when do non-linguistic factors dominate, such as shared sensory systems or common problem-solving demands? Researchers increasingly emphasize bidirectional influence. Culture and cognition shape language over time, while language, once learned, can feed back into cognition by stabilizing patterns of categorization that become socially shared. A practical implication is methodological caution. Demonstrating linguistic influence requires separating language effects from cultural practice, education, and task design. When experiments conflate these factors, determinism appears stronger than it is. A more credible conclusion is that language and thought are mutually influential: language provides tools that can sharpen or blur distinctions, but human cognition retains flexibility to operate beyond any one linguistic system.
